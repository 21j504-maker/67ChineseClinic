My Growing Pain

The most difficult part of the last 4-5 weeks of learning how to code a website has been transitioning from planning to actual code. The wireframing aspect of planning seemed easy because I could lay out concepts without worrying about whether something would “break.” However, the code aspect of planning required accuracy. A mistake as simple as forgetting to close a bracket or as complex as mislabeling a class or confusing how CSS interacts with HTML could cause the entire page to display incorrectly. When things weren’t working, my initial response was always frustration and self-doubt. Yet, rather than quitting, I began to learn to analyze problems more methodically and take my time. I went through my code line by line, using trial and error, and looked for examples. When I really couldn’t figure it out myself, I turned to my classmates or teacher. I began to see that “mistakes” are not reflections of failure but are actually part of the process of learning, and that every mistake I fixed led me to learn more about what the code was doing. What I learned from this is that I am more persistent than I thought. When I felt stuck, I kept trying different solutions. I also learned the fact that I learn more from problem-solving rather than when everything goes right. Coding taught me patience, resilience, and the importance of embracing challenges as opportunities to improve.
